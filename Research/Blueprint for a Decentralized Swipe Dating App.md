# Blueprint for a Decentralized Swipe Dating App

## Executive judgment

The most technically and commercially realistic version of your idea is **not** a pure serverless mobile dating app. The strongest design is a **hybrid decentralized architecture**: user profiles, photos, and messages live primarily on users’ own devices; a very small control plane helps with discovery, wakeups, and connection setup; direct device-to-device transfer is attempted first; and encrypted relays or user-chosen replica nodes are used only when direct delivery fails. That conclusion follows from four hard constraints in the current ecosystem: mobile OS background limits, NAT traversal reality, app-store rules for user-generated content, and the practical need for push notifications and fallback relays. Apple explicitly says background execution modes are limited and that apps are typically suspended in the background; Android similarly recommends optimizing apps to work without background processes entirely. Meanwhile, TURN exists precisely because direct peer-to-peer connections can be impossible in some NAT scenarios, and libp2p’s own relay docs make the same point in a decentralized stack. citeturn5search0turn6view0turn2view7turn15view1

So the winning plan is to build for **local-first ownership** rather than **absolute infrastructure absence**. Use the network only for presence, rendezvous, wakeups, and fallback routing; keep profile media and chat content off your servers by default; and make the first shipping version **online-only presence** so users are only discoverable while their device is reachable. That is the cleanest way to honor your decentralization goal without fighting iOS and Android at every layer. If you later want offline discoverability or offline delivery, add **encrypted delegated hosting** so users can choose their own replica devices or trusted storage peers. IPFS-style content addressing and pinning show how user-selected persistence can work, while local-first CRDT tools such as Automerge show how structured state can merge without a central database. citeturn15view2turn15view3turn15view4

## Reality check on decentralization and distribution

A full “everyone hosts their own profile all the time from their phone” model collides with mobile platform behavior. Apple documents that apps are typically suspended when in the background and only a limited set of background execution modes are allowed; Apple also warns that PushKit’s VoIP channel must not be used for anything other than actual VoIP calling. Android likewise documents that background processes are memory- and battery-intensive, and recommends optimizing apps to run without those background processes entirely. In plain English: phones are bad always-on servers, and iPhones are especially hostile to apps trying to pretend they are. citeturn5search0turn5search3turn6view1turn6view0

That makes your idea of “only show people who are available when they’re online” unusually sensible. It is not just a product choice; it is the architecture that best fits the operating systems. If a user is online, you can try direct peer-to-peer transfer. If the user goes offline, their profile simply drops out of the candidate pool unless they have explicitly chosen replica nodes or delegated storage. That is the cleanest starting point because it minimizes staleness, avoids background abuse, and reduces legal exposure from centrally hosted intimate data. This is an engineering inference from Apple’s and Android’s background limits, plus the fact that IPFS-style persistence requires some node to remain available or pinned. citeturn5search0turn6view0turn15view3turn18view0

The other harsh reality is distribution. Apple’s App Review Guidelines require filtering objectionable material, reporting, blocking, and reachable contact information for apps with user-generated content. Apple also says apps used primarily for pornographic content, random or anonymous chat, objectification of real people such as “hot-or-not” voting, or bullying do not belong on the App Store. Google Play likewise requires robust moderation, reporting, blocking, and default filtering for incidental sexual content, with minors restricted. So a philosophy of “absolutely no rules whatsoever” is not compatible with mainstream mobile storefronts, even if your content stays off your servers. citeturn3view3turn3view4turn3view6turn3view1turn3view2

If your real priority is maximal expressive freedom rather than App Store compliance, Apple practically tells you the path: “for everything else there is always the open Internet.” In other words, a **web app or progressive web app** is materially more compatible with your philosophy than a native iOS app. You can still ship native Android if you want, but any app distributed through Google Play must still meet Play’s UGC rules. A split strategy is therefore rational: a stricter native client for stores, and a more open web distribution channel for users willing to bypass store constraints. citeturn2view0turn3view3turn3view1

## Recommended architecture

### Control plane

Run a **minimal control plane** rather than a content-hosting backend. Its job should be limited to presence registration, coarse location buckets, connection bootstrap, abuse intake, and push-trigger orchestration. In libp2p terms, a federated rendezvous service is acceptable for this role; libp2p explicitly notes that Rendezvous is federated rather than decentralized, while DHT and Gossipsub are the decentralized alternatives. For a first version, the federated approach is the right trade because a tiny rendezvous system is much easier to operate than a phone-only DHT on mobile battery budgets. citeturn2view5turn15view0

The control plane should store as little as possible: a stable public key or peer ID, ephemeral session keys, coarse geography or search bucket, age-verification status flag, client capabilities, relay preferences, and the minimum signaling material needed for WebRTC or libp2p connection setup. For background wakeups, you will still need APNs and FCM plumbing, which in practice means some trusted server-side component. Apple documents background pushes and offline notification handling through APNs, and Firebase documents that FCM typically relies on a trusted environment such as an app server or Cloud Functions. That is not central content hosting; it is unavoidable delivery infrastructure. citeturn21search0turn21search5turn21search4turn21search16

For transport, use **libp2p over QUIC for native clients** and **WebRTC data channels for browser clients**. QUIC is standardized for low-latency, stream-multiplexed, encrypted transport, and libp2p recommends QUIC because of its faster handshake latency and better performance characteristics. If you expose a browser client, WebRTC data channels are the standard peer-to-peer data path. For NAT traversal, expect to use ICE/STUN first and TURN-like relay behavior when direct paths fail; Trickle ICE is worth adopting because it can considerably accelerate session establishment. citeturn16search0turn16search1turn16search7turn13search0turn13search1turn2view6turn2view7turn17search2

### Data plane

Make the app **local-first**. The authoritative profile record, preferences, full-resolution photos, block list, and chat history live on the user’s device by default. Small structured state such as profile fields, likes sent, match state, read receipts, and moderation labels can be replicated using a CRDT approach such as Automerge, which is explicitly designed for local-first software and automatic merging without a central server. Media blobs should not be shoved into a CRDT; use content-addressed blobs instead, because IPFS-style content addressing is a better fit for larger immutable objects. citeturn15view4turn15view2

A good separation is this: **records are mutable and replicated; media is immutable and content-addressed**. Each profile update publishes a signed manifest that references media object IDs, thumbnails, and capability metadata. When another user swipes through candidates, they fetch only the minimum derivative assets first. When two people match or one user opens a full profile, the client fetches higher-quality assets on demand. This design is an engineering recommendation, but it is directly motivated by the fact that IPFS-style pinning keeps immutable data available and that Automerge is meant for concurrent structured changes rather than heavy binary media. citeturn15view3turn15view4

If you want users to host each other’s data, do it **explicitly and cryptographically**. IPFS pinning exists to keep data available by exempting it from garbage collection, which is exactly what a volunteer replica network would be doing. But once somebody else pins a profile photo, that node is retaining the data persistently. So only replicate **encrypted blobs**, keep decryption keys under origin-user control, and rotate asset keys on delete or abuse takedown. That way, mirrors can retain encrypted garbage without being able to meaningfully expose it after revocation. The persistence behavior is documented by IPFS; the key-rotation advice is a design inference grounded in standard secure-storage practice. citeturn15view3turn18view0turn20search2turn20search3

### Identity and messaging

Use **passkeys for account authentication** and a separate on-device identity key for the peer layer. WebAuthn Level 3 defines public-key credentials for strong authentication, and FIDO describes passkeys as being built on FIDO2 specifications. For a dating app, passkeys are a much better anti-account-takeover baseline than passwords or SMS. On-device private keys should be protected with platform hardware where available, such as Android Keystore and Apple Secure Enclave. citeturn7search0turn7search1turn20search2turn20search3

For messaging, use an **asynchronous end-to-end encrypted prekey model**. Signal’s X3DH specification is still the clearest primary-source blueprint for establishing shared secrets between users who are not online at the same time. In practice, that means your minimal server or user-chosen mailbox peers can store only prekeys and encrypted envelopes, never plaintext chat. If you later add group chat, MLS is the current IETF standard for efficient asynchronous group key establishment, but for a swipe dating app, pairwise X3DH-style setup is the simpler starting point. citeturn7search3turn7search2

## Performance and media strategy

For latency, the best plan is to separate **discovery latency** from **media latency**. Discovery should move tiny signed records only: peer availability, coarse search attributes, a minimal card manifest, and one preview image derivative. Media should be pulled lazily and progressively. QUIC helps because it provides flow-controlled streams, low-latency connection establishment, and path migration, while Trickle ICE accelerates direct-connection setup by starting checks before all candidates are gathered. The architectural implication is straightforward: make the swipe feed work from very small manifests, and do not block the main interaction loop on full profile media. citeturn16search0turn17search2turn13search1

Your instinct about compression is directionally right, but the “10-bit minimum everywhere” requirement needs nuance. AVIF is based on AV1-in-HEIF and AV1 supports 8-, 10-, and 12-bit depths; Android 12 and higher natively support AVIF and Android’s own image guidance says AVIF dramatically improves image quality for the same file size versus older formats such as JPEG. Apple documents native HEIF/HEIC support for high-efficiency images, but in this research pass I did not find an Apple primary source that gives an equally clean, universal promise for AVIF app support across the whole current Apple client matrix. So the safest cross-platform strategy is: preserve the original asset locally, generate small swipe derivatives in the most efficient natively supported format on the target client, and reserve the highest-quality transfer for expanded profile view or post-match exchange. citeturn11view0turn11view2turn9view1turn8search2

If you insist on 10-bit as a product value, make it a requirement for **source assets and full-quality fetches**, not necessarily every feed preview. Swipe cards are small UI surfaces, and the bandwidth cost of always shipping 10-bit thumbnails is unlikely to pay for itself in user perception. That is an engineering inference from Android’s emphasis on reducing image download sizes, AVIF’s high-efficiency goal, and the practical limits of mobile transport. A good compromise is three tiers: a tiny preview derivative for feed browsing, a medium derivative for full profile view, and the original or near-original asset for explicit high-quality requests after interest is established. citeturn9view1turn16search0turn11view2

Do not assume pure peer-to-peer will always be faster than a relay. TURN exists because direct communication is sometimes impossible behind NAT, and libp2p circuit relays serve the same fallback function while preserving end-to-end encryption. So the right KPI is not “100% direct.” The right KPI is “direct when possible, graceful relay when necessary, and predictable swipe responsiveness regardless.” That mindset will keep you from over-optimizing for ideological purity at the expense of actual user experience. citeturn2view7turn15view1

## Safety, legality, and moderation design

A decentralized design reduces your custodianship of content, but it does **not** erase legal and platform obligations. Apple requires filtering, reporting, blocking, and contactability for UGC apps, and says apps used mainly for pornographic content, random or anonymous chat, or “hot-or-not” objectification do not belong on the App Store. Google Play requires robust moderation, reporting, blocking, and default filtering for incidental sexual content, with minors restricted from access. If you want mainstream mobile distribution, you need a real trust-and-safety model no matter where the bits live. citeturn3view3turn3view4turn3view1turn3view2

A better way to reconcile your “freedom first” instinct with reality is **composable moderation** rather than “no moderation.” AT Protocol’s labeling model is the best public example: moderation is split among network takedowns, labels from moderation services, and user controls such as mutes and blocks. You can borrow that pattern. The protocol can stay open, while clients subscribe to one or more labelers. Your official client can enforce the minimum needed for law and store compliance, while third-party clients can choose stricter or looser label sets where lawful. That preserves pluralism without pretending moderation does not exist. citeturn19search0turn19search2turn19search6

You also need to be realistic about criminal-edge risk. Apple’s safety rules explicitly call out hookup apps used to facilitate prostitution, human trafficking, or exploitation, and Congress amended Section 230 so it does not shield services that unlawfully promote or facilitate prostitution or sex trafficking. Separately, U.S. law imposes CyberTipline reporting duties on providers when apparent child sexual exploitation content is involved, to the extent the relevant information is within the provider’s custody or control. A decentralized design may reduce what is in your custody or control, but any discovery server, relay, abuse pipeline, or mailbox service you operate still creates obligations. citeturn3view3turn4search1turn1search2turn4search10

Because dating data is unusually sensitive, your privacy stance should be aggressive. The FTC’s guidance for app developers says not to collect or keep data you do not need, to use transit encryption, and to consider protecting data stored on users’ devices. That strongly supports your decentralization goal: avoid central photo storage, avoid precise location unless absolutely necessary, avoid indefinite retention, and protect local secrets with Android Keystore or Apple Secure Enclave. “Less data in your hands” is not just ideological purity; it is a security and compliance advantage. citeturn18view0turn20search2turn20search3

## Deployment roadmap

The first release should be an **online-only decentralized MVP**. Ship discovery through a minimal control plane, presence-based browsing, direct profile fetches, device-stored media, passkey login, on-device keys, blocking and reporting, and direct or relayed encrypted chat only after a match. Keep the discovery metadata tiny, and do not attempt offline browsing or communal phone-hosting of third-party media in v1. This gives you the highest chance of reaching a stable product without building a fragile science project. The recommendation is grounded in the mobile background limits, relay requirements, and local-first sync tools reviewed above. citeturn5search0turn6view0turn2view7turn15view4

The second release should add **delegated encrypted replication**. Let users nominate their own replica devices or trusted peers, or purchase third-party pinning through a standard interface, so their profile can remain available when their main device is offline. Because IPFS pinning is standardized and keeps data available by preventing garbage collection, it is a strong conceptual model here. But make replicas opt-in, encrypted, and revocable. That preserves your “I do not want to host everyone’s data” principle while giving users a clear tradeoff between privacy purity and availability. citeturn15view3turn14search12

The third release should introduce **open moderation infrastructure**. Keep your official client compliant with store and legal requirements, but publish a labeling interface so outside communities can define additional filters or alternate worldview packs. AT Protocol’s label architecture is a strong precedent for this layered approach. This is the most practical way to be “maximally free within legality” without guaranteeing an unusable or unshippable product. citeturn19search0turn19search2turn19search4

The final strategic decision is distributive, not technical: if you want the broadest native reach, accept store moderation constraints and ship a stricter official client. If you want the broadest expressive freedom, prioritize a web client and treat native mobile stores as constrained entry points rather than the center of gravity. Apple’s own guidance and App Review language make that tradeoff unusually explicit. citeturn2view0turn3view3
